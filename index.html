Section 1

Project Summary

The presentation positions The Old Bear as a robot built around controlled, repeatable scoring rather than aggressive shot-making. The design philosophy is explicit: move slowly, prioritize accuracy, and treat pushing as more dependable than shooting. That philosophy drives both the mechanical layout and the navigation logic described later in the deck.

From the extracted slides, the robot's operating cycle is straightforward and sequential. It first uses ultrasonics to determine its orientation, repositions itself into the home corner, strafes to the centre line, follows that line toward the hog line, deploys the puck with a pusher, then returns to repeat the cycle after reloading. The review presentation frames this loop as the main technical story of the project.

Section 3

Our Bot

CAD Assembly

One of the slides is dedicated to CAD assembly imagery, indicating that the mechanical packaging and physical integration of the robot were central to the team's review story. While the slide itself is image-heavy rather than text-heavy, it anchors the presentation's transition from team introduction to design intent.

In this site version, the CAD assembly portion is represented as a written bridge between the roster and the project philosophy: the robot is treated as a deliberately assembled platform whose mechanics exist to support accurate, repeated scoring cycles.

Slide Emphasis

The presentation does not dwell on decorative detail. It moves quickly from assembly visuals to the operating philosophy, which suggests the build was evaluated primarily by how reliably it executed the scoring cycle.

Design Philosophy

  • Slow and steady wins the race.
  • Prioritise accuracy. Pushing > shooting.
  • Mecanum drive allows for us to strafe rather than turn.

These three points are the clearest design thesis in the deck. They explain why the robot is not presented as a fast, dynamically aimed machine. Instead, it is framed as a controlled platform that reduces turning complexity, favors stable contact-based scoring, and uses lateral motion to simplify positioning in the arena.

Section 4

Overall Scoring Strategy

The strategy slide is the most operational part of the presentation. Rather than describing the robot in abstract terms, it lays out the cycle step by step. Presented as a sequence, the robot's behavior is easy to follow and clearly optimized for repeatability.

  1. Determine Rotation

    Use ultrasonics to figure out rotation of bot, and rotate accordingly.

  2. Return to the Home Corner

    Once facing forward, strafe left and move back to home to corner of arena.

  3. Acquire the Centre Line

    Strafe right until the centre line.

  4. Advance to the Hog Lines

    Follow centre line to hog lines.

  5. Deploy the Scoring Mechanism

    Drop puck, extend pusher, retract pusher (repeat 3x).

  6. Reverse the Path

    Follow centre line to back.

  7. Re-seat in the Corner

    Strafe left till back in corner.

  8. Reset and Repeat

    Wait 5 seconds, get reloaded, strafe to centre line, repeat.

Section 5

Outcomes

  • Super reliable for first scoring cycle.
  • Pusher worked great.
  • Line following left a bit to be desired, as the two front IR sensors were on the very edge of each side of the robot.

The presentation's results are notably candid. It claims a reliable first scoring cycle and a strong pusher mechanism, but it also explicitly calls out the weakest area: line following. The reason is not hand-waved. The deck ties that issue to front IR sensor placement at the edges of the chassis, which is exactly the kind of concrete hardware-software interaction that belongs in a final review.

Section 6

Lessons Learned

Key Errors

The final slide groups the team's retrospective under a direct heading: key errors.

  • Encoders on the drivetrain motors were not needed; the team eventually went for a sensor-based strategy.
  • Encoders on the turntable were needed and had to be calibrated based on battery voltage.
  • Before the tournament, the team never once tested multiple scoring cycles; the presentation calls this a mistake.
  • Assembly bottlenecks such as 3D printing and quick adjustments needed to be addressed much earlier.

This closing slide is the strongest evidence that the review deck is honest about tradeoffs and process failures, not just final performance. It distinguishes between subsystems that looked necessary but were ultimately unnecessary, subsystems that proved more critical than expected, and schedule problems that compounded integration risk late in the project.
